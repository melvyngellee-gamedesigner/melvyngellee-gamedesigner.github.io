My Story

My journey in the gaming world didn't begin in front of a screen, but around a table. Playing board games taught me to dissect mechanics before I ever touched a game engine: understanding the balance of rules, the importance of player chemistry, and the satisfaction of a well-oiled system. This curiosity naturally extended to video games as I grew up, where I learned to observe level architecture, environmental storytelling, and, of course, the mechanics themselves.

My approach to Game Design is the result of a balance between precision and emotion.

Passionate about the works of Tolkien and the density of universes like those of Cyberpunk 2077 or League of Legends, I see Narrative Design as complex architecture where even the smallest metaphorical detail reinforces immersion.

This pursuit of precision and emotion is also reflected in my inspirations, such as Fr√©d√©ric Chopin: from my Franco-Polish roots, I inherited a sensitivity for virtuoso complexity placed at the service of emotion. This translates even more into my approach to UX and Game Feel: an invisible technical complexity that allows the player to feel a pure and fluid emotion.

On the court, my experience with volleyball gave me a sharp understanding of collective dynamics and the transmission of shared emotions. Whether through the mechanical depth of Rocket League or the strategic demands of roguelikes, I strive to seek experiences where system precision meets the satisfaction of pushing one's limits.

My goal is simple: to design rich worlds and deep mechanics that, once in hand, transform every session into an unforgettable moment of synergy.

"How to align 10 brains on an invisible mechanic?"

Team of 10 3 Months Unity & Fmod

00. Context & Origins

Chromesthesia represents a significant milestone in my academic journey as an end-of-year project that brought together three distinct curriculums for the first time.

01. The Challenge: A Silent Mechanic

The game is a puzzle-platformer regarding perception. The player manipulates sound frequencies to make platforms appear. The core challenge was communication: how do we make sound (invisible) a tangible gameplay element for the player?

Working with 6 Game Designers and 2 Sound Designers, my role shifted from pure design to technical mediation. We had ideas, but implementing them required a rigorous bridge between creative intent and the engine.

BLOODLUST

VISCERAL ACTION BRAWLER

"Making the player feel every hit through Code and Sound."

Team of 5 Combat Design Game Feel

01. The Goal: "Juice" above all

Bloodlust had one simple premise: pure, unadulterated violence. But "violence" in games is about timing and feedback.

As the Lead Sound Designer and Combat Designer, my obsession was the "Game Feel". I didn't want the player to just see the attack; I wanted them to feel the impact.

DEFINING "THE CRUNCH" (DOC)

My analysis on Hitstop, Screen Shake, and Audio Feedback.

02. Audio Design & Integration

In a beat 'em up, hearing the exact same punch sound a thousand times causes listener fatigue. My role was to curate specific sounds that matched the gameplay utility (hit validation, finishers) and ensure their coherence with the visual impact.

Implementation: Using Visual Scripting, I integrated these assets directly into the engine. I created logic graphs to randomize pitch and volume on every hit, ensuring variety and organic feedback without needing hundreds of different sound files.

UNGOR BOK

SOLO PROJECT - ONE BUTTON GAME

"How much depth can you create with a single input?"

Solo Dev C# Programming Constraints

01. The Constraint as a Muse

Ungor Bok was born from a strict constraint: One Button Only. No directional keys, no jump button. Just SPACE.

This forced me to strip Game Design down to its absolute core: Timing. I chose a grappling hook mechanic because it offers momentum and physics-based gameplay that feels rewarding to master.

THE CONCEPT (ONE PAGE)

Synthesizing a complex physics idea into a simple document.

02. The UX Problem

The Struggle: Without directional keys, players felt helpless aiming the hook.

The Solution: I coded a rotating arrow around the character (diegetic crosshair). The player doesn't control the direction directly, but they control when to shoot based on the arrow's rotation. It turned frustration into a rhythm game.

03. Full Autonomy

Being a solo developer meant I had to engineer my own ideas. I wrote the state machine for the character physics and the raycast logic for the hook.

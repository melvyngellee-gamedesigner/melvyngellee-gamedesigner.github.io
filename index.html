My Story

My journey in the gaming world didn't begin in front of a screen, but around a table. Playing board games taught me to dissect mechanics before I ever touched a game engine: understanding the balance of rules, the importance of player chemistry, and the satisfaction of a well-oiled system. This curiosity naturally extended to video games as I grew up, where I learned to observe level architecture, environmental storytelling, and, of course, the mechanics themselves.

My approach to Game Design is the result of a balance between precision and emotion.

Passionate about the works of Tolkien and the density of universes like those of Cyberpunk 2077 or League of Legends, I see Narrative Design as complex architecture where even the smallest metaphorical detail reinforces immersion.

This pursuit of precision and emotion is also reflected in my inspirations, such as Frédéric Chopin: from my Franco-Polish roots, I inherited a sensitivity for virtuoso complexity placed at the service of emotion. This translates even more into my approach to UX and Game Feel: an invisible technical complexity that allows the player to feel a pure and fluid emotion.

On the court, my experience with volleyball gave me a sharp understanding of collective dynamics and the transmission of shared emotions. Whether through the mechanical depth of Rocket League or the strategic demands of roguelikes, I strive to seek experiences where system precision meets the satisfaction of pushing one's limits.

My goal is simple: to design rich worlds and deep mechanics that, once in hand, transform every session into an unforgettable moment of synergy.

CHROMESTESIA

2.5D PLATFORMER

"Comment synchroniser 10 cerveaux sur une mécanique invisible ?"

Team of 10 3 Months Unity & Fmod

01. The Challenge: A Silent Mechanic

Chromesthesia isn't just a platformer; it's a puzzle regarding perception. The player creates platforms using sound frequencies. The core challenge was communication: how do we make sound (invisible) a tangible gameplay element for the player?

Working with 6 Game Designers and 2 Sound Designers, my role shifted from pure design to technical mediation. We had great ideas, but implementing them required a rigorous bridge between the creative intent and the engine.

THE INITIAL VISION (PITCH DECK)

Discover how we sold the concept of "Sound as Matter".

02. Bridging the Gap: Rational Level Design

One of the hardest hurdles was visualizing the flow. On paper (Miro), the levels looked great. In Unity, the pacing felt off. I took charge of the Rational Level Design.

I realized that relying solely on intuition wasn't enough for a team of 10. We needed math to verify our feelings. I created balancing matrices to calculate the "Action/Rest" ratio of our levels before even placing a single block in Unity.

03. What I learned

This project taught me that documentation is a design tool, not just paperwork. By structuring our data clearly on Excel and Miro, I helped the Sound Designers and Level Designers speak the same language, avoiding weeks of rework.

BLOODLUST

VISCERAL ACTION BRAWLER

"Making the player feel every hit through Code and Sound."

Team of 5 Combat Design Game Feel

01. The Goal: "Juice" above all

Bloodlust had one simple premise: pure, unadulterated violence. But "violence" in games isn't just about red pixels; it's about timing and feedback.

As the Lead Sound Designer and Combat Designer, my obsession was the "Game Feel". I didn't want the player to just see the attack; I wanted them to feel the impact.

DEFINING "THE CRUNCH" (DOC)

My analysis on Hitstop, Screen Shake, and Audio Feedback.

02. The Technical Wall: Adaptive Audio

The main issue we faced was repetition. In a Brawler, you hit things thousands of times. Standard sound effects became annoying very quickly.

My solution: I leveraged my C# knowledge to build an adaptive audio system. Instead of playing one sound, I coded a randomizer for pitch and volume variations on every hit. I also implemented a "Dynamic Intensity" system where the music layers build up based on the combo counter.

This technical implementation of a creative need is what I'm most proud of on this project.

03. Organizing the Chaos (System Design)

Beyond the feel, a Brawler needs structure. I managed the System Design for the enemy waves. Using Excel, I balanced the HP, damage, and spawn rates to create a flow state—challenging but never unfair.

UNGOR BOK

SOLO PROJECT - ONE BUTTON GAME

"How much depth can you create with a single input?"

Solo Dev C# Programming Constraints

01. The Constraint as a Muse

Ungor Bok was born from a strict constraint: One Button Only. No directional keys, no jump button. Just SPACE.

This forced me to strip Game Design down to its absolute core: Timing. I chose a grappling hook mechanic because it offers momentum and physics-based gameplay that feels rewarding to master, even with limited inputs.

THE CONCEPT (ONE PAGE)

Synthesizing a complex physics idea into a simple document.

02. The UX Problem

The Struggle: During the first playtests, players were frustrated. Without directional keys, they couldn't aim the hook. They felt helpless.

The Solution: I couldn't add buttons, so I added visual information. I coded a rotating arrow around the character. It acts as a diegetic crosshair. The player doesn't control the direction directly, but they control when to shoot based on the arrow's rotation. It turned frustration into a rhythm game.

03. Full Autonomy (Coding)

Being a solo developer on this meant I had to be the engineer of my own ideas. I wrote the state machine for the character physics and the raycast logic for the hook.

This project honed my ability to prototype fast. If I have an idea, I don't just write it down; I open Unity and build it to see if it's fun.
